## Module 1: What is Bending Spoons?

### Origin Story: Five Friends in Copenhagen

Bending Spoons was founded in **2013 in Milan, Italy** by five co-founders who met while studying in Copenhagen, Denmark: **Luca Ferrari** (CEO), **Francesco Patarnello**, **Matteo Danieli**, **Luca Querella**, and **Tomasz Greber**. Before Bending Spoons, the group had co-founded an unsuccessful startup called **Evertale**, a note-taking app. When Evertale failed, they had roughly **$40,000 in seed capital** left over — and that became the entire starting fund for what would become an $18 billion company.

The company's name comes from the iconic scene in *The Matrix* where a child bends a spoon with their mind — signaling a mindset of reshaping the digital market through rigorous thinking and a refusal to accept conventional limitations. It was a fitting name for a company that would go on to challenge every assumption about how software companies should be run.

### The Early Years: Mobile App Experiments (2013–2021)

In its first phase, Bending Spoons operated as a **bootstrapped mobile app studio**. The founders built simple utility and entertainment apps — not as products to scale individually, but as **live experiments for proprietary marketing and monetization algorithms**. Each app was a test bed. Which pricing model converts best? Which acquisition channel yields the highest LTV? What onboarding flow minimizes churn? The data from dozens of small apps fed a growing engine of operational knowledge.

One foundational product was **Splice**, a video editing app launched in the mid-2010s that the company describes as "the app that started it all." Splice became a top-ranked app on the App Store and demonstrated that Bending Spoons could take a commodity product category and win through superior execution on monetization and user experience optimization.

Another breakout was **Remini**, an AI-powered photo enhancement app. Bending Spoons didn't just iterate on Remini — they **rewrote every line of code**, rebuilt it from the ground up, and turned it into a top-ranked App Store download. The Remini playbook — acquire or build, then ruthlessly rebuild and optimize — became the template for everything that followed.

💡 Key Insight

Bending Spoons was **profitable from day one**. They never took venture capital during their first nine years. It wasn't until 2022 — nearly a decade after founding — that they raised their first significant equity round. This bootstrapped discipline shapes everything about how they operate.

### The Pivot: From App Studio to Acquisition Machine (2022–Present)

The transition happened in late 2022 when Bending Spoons made its first major acquisition: **Evernote**, the once-iconic note-taking app that had fallen from its unicorn heights into a state of bloat, declining revenue, and organizational drift. This wasn't a typical tech acquisition. Bending Spoons didn't buy Evernote for its team or its technology stack — they bought it for its **brand, its user base, and its subscription revenue**.

What happened next shocked the tech industry. Within weeks, Bending Spoons **laid off nearly the entire Evernote staff**, relocated operations to Europe, and began rebuilding the product with a fraction of the original headcount. Then they started raising prices — dramatically. Evernote's annual subscription went from roughly **$70/year to $250/year by 2026**, a 257% increase. And the product kept running. And subscribers, while they complained, largely stayed.

The Evernote acquisition proved the thesis: you could buy a mature SaaS product, eliminate most of the cost structure, raise prices, and the revenue would not only survive but **grow**. That playbook has now been applied over 50 times.

### How Ferrari Sees Bending Spoons

CEO Luca Ferrari describes Bending Spoons as **"25% private equity and 75% technology company."** This framing is important. Unlike pure financial buyers like Vista Equity or Thoma Bravo, Bending Spoons isn't just applying financial engineering to software assets. They're applying a **proprietary technology platform and operational methodology** that they believe creates value no financial buyer can replicate.

The company's core thesis is that most software companies are dramatically **overstaffed relative to the value they deliver**. A product with 500 employees often needs only 50 — or even 20 — if you strip away organizational complexity, eliminate non-essential features, automate routine operations with AI, and focus relentlessly on the core value proposition that keeps users paying. This is radical in an industry that typically measures success by headcount growth. Bending Spoons measures success by **revenue per employee**.

### The Funding Journey: From $40K to $18B

Understanding Bending Spoons' funding history reveals the strategic evolution of the company. For nearly a decade (2013–2022), the founders ran the company on bootstrapped profits — no venture capital, no private equity, no outside board members telling them what to do. This period forged the operational discipline that defines the company today. When you build a company with $40,000 and no outside funding, you learn to be ruthlessly efficient with every dollar, every hire, every line of code.

The funding timeline accelerated dramatically starting in 2024:

The trajectory from $40,000 to $18.4 billion in 13 years — with the first outside capital arriving only in year 11 — is one of the most remarkable wealth creation stories in European tech history. It also explains the founders' enormous ownership stake and their insistence on the dual-class share structure: they built this company themselves, with their own money, for nearly a decade before letting anyone else in.

### Why This Matters for Tech Leaders

Bending Spoons matters to VPs of Engineering and CTOs for a simple reason: **they have proven that the conventional wisdom about how many people it takes to run a software product is wrong**. Whether you admire or deplore their methods, the results are undeniable. Products that employed hundreds of people continue to operate — and in some cases generate more revenue — with a fraction of the original staff. This challenges every assumption about team sizing, organizational design, and the relationship between headcount and product quality that most engineering leaders take for granted.

As you work through this course, the question isn't whether you should adopt the Bending Spoons model wholesale. The question is: **what specific mechanisms are they using to achieve 5–10x efficiency, and which of those mechanisms could you apply in your own context?**

## Module 2: The F-1 / IPO Deep Dive

### The Filing: What We Know

On **June 8, 2026**, Bending Spoons filed a **Form F-1** with the SEC — the foreign private issuer equivalent of an S-1 — for an initial public offering on the **Nasdaq Global Select Market** under the ticker symbol **BSP**. The IPO priced on **July 1, 2026 at $29 per share**, above the initial $26–$28 target range, reflecting strong investor demand.

The offering raised **$1.68 billion** in total. Of that, 34,398,640 shares were new shares issued by the company, and 23,572,375 shares were sold by existing shareholders — a mix of primary and secondary that signals both company capital needs and early investor liquidity.

The IPO valued the company at approximately **$18.4 billion**, representing a **67% increase** from the $11 billion valuation achieved just eight months earlier in the October 2025 private round.

### Why F-1 and Not S-1?

Because Bending Spoons is incorporated outside the United States (it's an Italian company), it files as a **foreign private issuer** using Form F-1 rather than the S-1 used by U.S. domestic companies. This distinction matters: foreign private issuers have **fewer ongoing reporting requirements** than domestic issuers. They file annual reports on Form 20-F instead of 10-K, and they're not required to file quarterly 10-Q reports or proxy statements. For investors, this means **less visibility** into operations between annual reports.

### Dual-Class Share Structure

The F-1 establishes a **dual-class share structure** with Class A shares carrying **5 votes each**. These Class A shares are held exclusively by the four co-founders: Matteo Danieli, Luca Ferrari, Francesco Patarnello, and Luca Querella. Public investors receive ordinary shares with one vote each. This means the co-founders maintain **effective control of the company** regardless of their economic ownership percentage — a structure common among founder-led tech companies (Google, Meta, Snap) but controversial among governance advocates.

The F-1 states that proceeds will be used for **general corporate purposes and new acquisitions** — notably **NOT for deleveraging**. This tells us the company plans to continue its aggressive acquisition strategy even after going public, rather than using IPO proceeds to pay down its $4.4 billion in debt. The implication is that Bending Spoons believes the return on deploying capital into new acquisitions exceeds the cost of carrying its current debt load.

## Module 3: The Acquisition Playbook

### Target Identification: What Bending Spoons Looks For

Bending Spoons has a clearly defined acquisition target profile. They look for what Ferrari calls **"good but broken" companies** — products that share several characteristics:

-   **Established brand with loyal user base:** The product must have meaningful brand recognition and a sticky installed base of users or subscribers. Bending Spoons isn't interested in building awareness — they want products people already know and use.
-   **Proven subscription revenue:** Recurring revenue from paying users provides the predictable cash flow that supports the debt-funded acquisition model.
-   **Bloated cost structure:** The target company should be significantly overstaffed relative to its revenue, with large engineering teams, excessive middle management, and redundant corporate functions. This is the fat that Bending Spoons cuts.
-   **Stagnant or declining growth:** Companies that have plateaued are often available at distressed valuations (1–3x revenue) and may have boards or founders who are exhausted and ready to exit.
-   **Untapped pricing power:** Products that have been underpriced or have never tested aggressive pricing strategies — often because the previous leadership was afraid of churn.

### Deal Strategy: Bilateral, Not Auction

Bending Spoons typically avoids competitive auction processes. Instead, they pursue **bilateral deals** — approaching companies directly, often before they've formally put themselves up for sale. They're willing to pay **significant premiums** to close deals quickly and decisively: the Brightcove deal came at a 90% premium over the 60-day VWAP, Vimeo at 91%, and Eventbrite at 82%. These premiums, which might look expensive at first glance, are strategic — they make boards say yes quickly and discourage other bidders from competing.

### The Pattern: Buy → Cut → Optimize → Grow

Every major acquisition follows a remarkably consistent four-phase pattern:

#### Phase 1: Close & Assess (Weeks 1–2)

Immediately after closing, Bending Spoons deploys a small team to assess the acquired company's entire operation: code quality, infrastructure costs, team structure, revenue per feature, customer support volume, and pricing elasticity. This assessment period is measured in **weeks, not months**.

#### Phase 2: Cut (Weeks 2–8)

The most visible — and controversial — phase. Headcount reductions of **50–85%** are standard. The cuts are deep, fast, and across every function. Engineering teams that numbered in the hundreds are reduced to double digits. Marketing, HR, finance, customer support, and corporate functions are either eliminated entirely or absorbed into Bending Spoons' shared services in Milan.

#### Phase 3: Rebuild & Optimize (Months 2–6)

The remaining team, supplemented by Bending Spoons engineers from Milan, migrates the product onto Bending Spoons' shared technology platform. Features that don't drive revenue are killed. The tech stack is modernized. AI is deployed for customer support, content moderation, and bug detection. Pricing is restructured — almost always upward.

#### Phase 4: Steady-State (Ongoing)

The product enters "maintenance and growth" mode with a skeleton crew. A product that previously had 300 employees might now run with 15–30. The freed-up cash flow is reinvested into the next acquisition.

### Case Studies: Before and After

📚 Case Study: Evernote

**Before (2022):** ~400 employees, ~$100M ARR, declining growth, bloated feature set, aging tech stack, demoralized team. Annual subscription ~$70/year.

**After (2026):** Skeleton crew, relocated to Europe. Annual subscription raised to ~$250/year (257% increase). Product simplified. Core note-taking functionality maintained. Revenue per employee dramatically higher. User forums show complaints about reduced support but the product continues to function and subscribers continue to pay.

📚 Case Study: WeTransfer

**Before (Jul 2024):** 80M MAU, 600K subscribers, ~full staff. Had previously attempted its own IPO at ~$776M valuation that fell through.

**After (Sep 2024):** 75% of staff laid off within two months. Free plan terms restricted. Operations consolidated to Milan. The speed of the cuts — less than 90 days from close to restructuring — illustrates the playbook's ruthless efficiency.

📚 Case Study: Vimeo

**Before (Nov 2025):** 1,000+ employees globally, publicly traded on Nasdaq, significant Israeli engineering operations ($200M+ in acquisition-built tech operations), comprehensive video platform.

**After (Jan 2026):** "Almost everyone" laid off on January 20, 2026, including the entire video engineering team. Israeli operations wound down completely. Delisted from Nasdaq. ## Module 4: The Post-Acquisition Operating Model

### The "Universal OS": Bending Spoons' Secret Weapon

At the heart of Bending Spoons' operating model is what they internally call the **"Universal OS"** — a shared platform for engineering, marketing, billing, customer support, and analytics that every acquired product is migrated onto. This is not just a shared services center; it's a **complete operational stack** that replaces the acquired company's entire back office, infrastructure, and most of its engineering organization.

When Bending Spoons acquires a company, they don't try to integrate the acquired company's systems into their own. They **replace them entirely**. The acquired company's billing system, CRM, customer support tools, analytics pipeline, CI/CD infrastructure, monitoring stack — all of it gets ripped out and replaced by Bending Spoons' centralized platform. This is why they can cut 70–85% of headcount: most of those people were maintaining systems that Bending Spoons already has.

#### What the Universal OS Includes

Universal OS Components

##### Shared Finance & HR

Centralized in Milan. Eliminates acquired company's entire finance, legal, HR, and admin teams.

##### Shared Data Science

Centralized analytics and experimentation platform. A/B testing infrastructure, pricing optimization algorithms, churn prediction models.

##### Shared Infrastructure

Cloud infrastructure, CI/CD pipelines, monitoring, alerting — all standardized across the portfolio.

### Day 1 Playbook: What Happens Immediately After Closing

The first days and weeks after an acquisition closes follow a well-rehearsed script:

#### Week 1: The Assessment Sprint

A small team of Bending Spoons operators — typically 5–10 people — arrives (virtually or physically) to conduct a rapid assessment. They're examining several critical dimensions: What does the code look like? How much technical debt exists? Which features drive revenue and which don't? What's the customer support volume and what percentage could be automated? What's the real cost structure, line by line? How elastic is pricing — how much can they raise prices before churn accelerates?

#### Weeks 2–4: The Restructuring

Based on the assessment, restructuring begins. This is the phase that generates headlines. Severance packages are offered — in the Meetup case, impacted employees received **16 weeks of pay and up to 12 months of health insurance**. The cuts are typically deepest in engineering (where Bending Spoons' shared platform replaces most functions), corporate (finance, HR, legal), and customer support (where AI takes over). Product and design teams are also dramatically reduced.

#### Months 1–3: The Migration

The remaining product team — often just 10–30 people — begins migrating the product onto Bending Spoons' shared infrastructure. Legacy code is assessed for rewriting. The company has an **obsession with modern code** during this phase because, as they've discovered, newer code works significantly better with AI generation tools. If the codebase is too old or too tangled, they'll rewrite entire subsystems rather than try to maintain them.

#### Months 3–6: Pricing and Monetization Optimization

Once the product is stabilized on the new infrastructure, the monetization team goes to work. This typically involves some combination of: raising subscription prices, restricting or eliminating free tiers, adding new premium features, optimizing the conversion funnel from free to paid, and running extensive A/B tests on pricing, packaging, and positioning. Ferrari describes the learning phase as lasting **"several weeks to a couple of months"** before they have enough data to make major pricing moves.

### Product Rationalization: Kill Features, Focus on Core Value

Alongside headcount cuts, Bending Spoons applies a ruthless approach to **feature rationalization**. Their philosophy is that most mature software products have accumulated years of feature bloat — features that were added to satisfy specific enterprise customers, features that were someone's pet project, features that sounded good in a product review but are used by less than 1% of users.

Bending Spoons systematically measures which features drive retention and revenue and which don't. Features that don't justify their maintenance cost are eliminated. This simplifies the codebase, reduces the engineering burden, and often **actually improves the product** for the majority of users by reducing complexity. Of course, the minority of users who relied on those features are left frustrated — and vocal about it on social media.

### Pricing Optimization: The Bold Bet

Bending Spoons' pricing philosophy is rooted in a counterintuitive insight: **most software products are significantly underpriced**. The reasoning goes like this: if you have a product with high switching costs and strong user habit loops, the ceiling on what users will pay is much higher than what most companies charge. Previous leadership teams were afraid to test this ceiling because they feared churn. Bending Spoons isn't afraid of churn — they're optimizing for **revenue, not user count**.

The Evernote pricing trajectory illustrates this perfectly. Going from $70/year to $250/year means you can lose **72% of your subscribers** and still make the same revenue. In practice, far fewer subscribers churned. The users who stayed — the ones who genuinely rely on the product daily — turned out to have much higher willingness to pay than the previous team had ever tested.

💡 The Pricing Math

If Evernote had 1M subscribers at $70/year = $70M ARR. At $250/year, they only need 280K subscribers to match that revenue. Even if 50% of subscribers churned (leaving 500K), revenue would increase to $125M — a 79% increase in ARR while losing half the user base. This math is the core of the Bending Spoons thesis on pricing.

### The Ferrari Framework: "25% PE, 75% Tech Company"

Ferrari's self-description is more than a soundbite — it captures something real about how Bending Spoons operates differently from pure financial buyers. The 25% PE component is the financial engineering: identifying undervalued assets, using leverage to acquire them, cutting costs to improve margins. But the 75% tech component is the differentiator: the Universal OS, the AI automation, the shared engineering platform, the proprietary data science capabilities. A PE firm can cut headcount, but it can't replace those employees with an automated platform the way Bending Spoons can.

This is also why Ferrari insists Bending Spoons never sells an acquired business. PE firms buy, optimize, and flip. Bending Spoons buys, rebuilds, and **holds forever**. Every product in the portfolio generates cash flow that funds the next acquisition. The flywheel accelerates with each deal because the Universal OS becomes more capable and efficient with every product it absorbs.

### The Flywheel Effect: Why Each Acquisition Makes the Next One Easier

Perhaps the most strategically powerful aspect of Bending Spoons' model is its **compounding nature**. Each acquisition doesn't just add revenue — it strengthens the entire operating platform in ways that make the next acquisition cheaper to integrate and faster to optimize. Here's how the flywheel works:

-   **Platform investment amortizes across more products:** Every improvement to the Universal OS — a better billing engine, a smarter AI support bot, a more efficient deployment pipeline — benefits all 50+ products simultaneously. The fixed cost of maintaining the platform is spread across an ever-growing revenue base.
-   **Operational playbook gets refined:** Each acquisition teaches the team something new about post-acquisition integration. The Evernote integration informed the Meetup playbook, which informed WeTransfer, which informed Vimeo. By the 15th acquisition, the team can execute the playbook in their sleep.
-   **AI automation improves with data:** More products mean more data to train AI models on. Support patterns from Evernote improve the AI support bot for Meetup. Pricing algorithms tested on WeTransfer inform pricing for Komoot. The AI layer gets smarter with each product added.
-   **Cash flow compounds:** Each optimized product generates recurring cash flow that funds the next acquisition. As the portfolio grows, the cash flow available for acquisitions grows proportionally — enabling larger and more frequent deals.
-   **Talent density increases:** The best engineers and operators self-select into Bending Spoons because the scale and variety of the challenge is unique. Each successful integration builds the team's confidence and capability.

This flywheel is the fundamental reason Bending Spoons believes it can sustain its acquisition pace. The model isn't just additive (more products = more revenue); it's **multiplicative** (more products = better platform = faster integration = higher margins = more capital = more products). Whether this virtuous cycle can continue indefinitely or eventually hits natural limits is one of the key questions for the company's future.

### What the Model Means for Acquired Employees

It's worth spending a moment on the human reality of the operating model, because understanding the employee experience is essential for anyone evaluating whether and how to apply these lessons in their own organization.

When Bending Spoons acquires your company, the timeline is roughly:

-   **Day 1:** Announcement email from Bending Spoons leadership. Reassuring language about the transition.
-   **Days 2–14:** Assessment period. Bending Spoons operators study the codebase, the team structure, the financials. Some employees are asked to provide extensive documentation of their work and systems.
-   **Weeks 2–6:** Restructuring announcements. For many employees, this is when they learn their position is being eliminated. In the Meetup case, impacted employees received 16 weeks of severance pay and up to 12 months of continued health insurance — relatively generous by tech industry standards. In other cases, the terms have been less clear publicly.
-   **Months 2–6:** Surviving employees (typically 15–30% of the original staff) work to migrate the product onto Bending Spoons' infrastructure. Some are retained permanently as part of the Bending Spoons core team. Others leave after the transition period.

The pattern is well-known enough in the tech industry that employees at potential Bending Spoons targets sometimes begin job searching **before the acquisition is even announced** — simply based on rumors that Bending Spoons is in discussions with their company.

## Module 5: Engineering & Technology Philosophy

### The Small Team Thesis

Bending Spoons operates with **extraordinarily small engineering teams** per product — often fewer than 20 engineers maintaining products with millions of users. This isn't a bug or a cost-cutting side effect; it's a **core architectural principle**. Ferrari and his team believe that beyond a certain threshold (which they place much lower than most tech companies), additional engineers create negative marginal returns: more coordination overhead, more meetings, more code review bottlenecks, more merge conflicts, more architectural complexity, more institutional knowledge to maintain.

The core Bending Spoons team numbers approximately **804 employees as of May 2026** — this is the operational hub in Milan that runs the entire portfolio of 50+ products serving 500M+ monthly users. When you include staff at recently acquired subsidiaries who haven't yet been transitioned, the total headcount is closer to 4,600–5,400, but this number declines continuously as post-acquisition restructuring completes.

### AI-First Development

Bending Spoons is one of the most aggressive adopters of AI in software engineering operations. They estimate that **approximately 80% of routine tasks** are now automated using AI:

AI Automation Across the Stack

##### Bug Detection & Triage

AI systems automatically detect, classify, and prioritize bugs. Many minor bugs are fixed automatically without human intervention.

##### Customer Support

AI handles ~80% of customer support inquiries across all products. Humans only handle edge cases and escalations.

##### Content Moderation

For platforms like Meetup and Eventbrite, AI handles content moderation at scale without human moderators.

##### Code Generation & Review

Engineers leverage AI tools for code generation, and the company's obsession with modern code is partly because newer codebases work better with AI tools.

##### Pricing & A/B Testing

AI-driven experimentation runs pricing, packaging, and UX tests continuously across the portfolio.

##### Infrastructure Management

Automated scaling, deployment, and monitoring across the Universal OS platform.

### The Modern Code Obsession

One of the less obvious but strategically important aspects of Bending Spoons' technology approach is their **obsession with rewriting acquired codebases in modern tech stacks**. When they acquire a product with a legacy codebase, they don't just maintain it — they rebuild it. The Remini example is illustrative: they rewrote **every single line of code**.

Why? Because modern code works dramatically better with AI generation tools. A codebase written in modern TypeScript with well-defined APIs and standard patterns can be extended, debugged, and maintained by AI tools far more effectively than a legacy Java monolith with 15 years of accumulated technical debt. By investing upfront in modernizing codebases, Bending Spoons reduces the ongoing human engineering effort required to maintain and evolve each product.

This creates a virtuous cycle: modernize the code → AI can do more → fewer engineers needed → lower costs → higher margins → more capital for acquisitions → more products to modernize → AI platform gets smarter.

### Cloud Infrastructure & AI Platform

Bending Spoons has built a sophisticated AI infrastructure platform, developed in partnership with Google Cloud. The platform includes:

-   **Virtual machines on powerful GPUs** for AI model training and inference
-   **Hyperparameter tuning infrastructure** for optimizing AI models across the portfolio
-   **Cloud storage and data warehousing** for centralized analytics across all products
-   **Low-latency prediction serving** for real-time AI features (like Remini's photo enhancement)
-   **Shared experimentation platform** for A/B testing across the entire product portfolio

The Remini AI photo enhancement app, which has topped App Store rankings globally, is perhaps the best demonstration of Bending Spoons' AI capabilities. The app uses deep learning models to enhance and restore photos, and it was built and scaled by a team that is a fraction of the size you'd expect for an app with over 100 million users.

### Team Structure: Autonomous Pods

Engineers at Bending Spoons work in **small, autonomous, result-oriented teams** alongside product managers, growth managers, designers, and data/AI researchers. There's minimal hierarchy. A typical product team for a major brand like Evernote or Meetup might consist of:

-   1 Product Manager
-   5–12 Engineers (full-stack, mobile, backend)
-   1–2 Designers
-   1–2 Data Scientists / AI Researchers
-   1 Growth Manager

That's 10–18 people running a product that might have previously had 200–400 employees. The shared services (infrastructure, billing, support, analytics, finance, HR) sit outside the product team and serve the entire portfolio.

### The Code Rewrite Philosophy: A Deep Dive

Most software engineering leaders follow the conventional wisdom that **rewrites are almost always a mistake**. Joel Spolsky's famous essay "Things You Should Never Do" argues that rewriting code from scratch throws away years of accumulated knowledge embodied in bug fixes, edge case handling, and hard-won optimizations. This wisdom is generally correct — but Bending Spoons has found a specific context where rewrites make strategic sense.

Their reasoning chain goes like this: (1) Modern code works dramatically better with AI tools than legacy code. A well-structured TypeScript codebase with clear APIs can be navigated, modified, and extended by AI coding assistants far more effectively than a 15-year-old Java monolith with undocumented conventions and tangled dependencies. (2) AI-assisted maintenance is the only way to run products with 10-20 engineers that previously required 100-200. (3) Therefore, the upfront cost of a rewrite is an investment in permanently lower operating costs. (4) And because they've already eliminated the original engineering team, they're not throwing away institutional knowledge — it's already gone. The rewrite is building new institutional knowledge in a form that both humans and AI can work with effectively.

This is a genuinely novel insight about the economics of software rewrites in the AI era. The traditional calculus assumed that maintenance would always be done by humans, making institutional knowledge encoded in legacy code valuable. When maintenance is increasingly done by AI, the value of legacy code drops and the value of clean, modern, well-documented code rises dramatically.

### Engineering Decision-Making: Data Over Intuition

Bending Spoons applies the same data-driven rigor to engineering decisions that they apply to pricing and product decisions. Every major engineering choice — should we rewrite this service? Should we migrate to a different framework? Should we build this feature or kill it? — is backed by **quantitative analysis**. How much does this code cost to maintain (measured by engineering hours per month)? How many support tickets does this feature generate? What's the revenue impact of this feature based on A/B testing? How much would the rewrite cost, and what's the expected reduction in ongoing maintenance?

Ferrari famously described killing a $7 million project to avoid sunk-cost fallacy: rather than continuing to invest in a failing initiative because of what they'd already spent, the team analyzed the expected return on further investment, determined it was negative, and shut it down immediately. This kind of cold-blooded analytical decision-making, applied consistently across engineering investments, is a key enabler of the small-team model. Every engineering hour is treated as a scarce resource to be allocated to its highest-return use.

💬 CEO Quote

"Calculate boldly, but act rationally. We killed a $7 million project because the math said continuing was worse than stopping. Most companies can't do that because of ego and politics. We can because we only care about the numbers." — Luca Ferrari

### Custom Backend Generation

One specific technical capability that Bending Spoons has developed is the ability to **rapidly generate custom backends** for acquired products. Their standardized tech stack and tooling means that migrating an acquired product's backend onto Bending Spoons' infrastructure is a semi-automated process. What might take a typical company months of backend rewriting can be done in weeks because the patterns, APIs, and infrastructure are already in place.

💡 What This Means for VPs of Engineering

Bending Spoons' engineering approach challenges the assumption that complex products require large teams. Their model suggests that the right combination of **modern codebases + AI tooling + shared infrastructure + small autonomous teams** can maintain and evolve products at a fraction of the traditional headcount. Whether this is sustainable long-term without degrading product quality is the key open question.

### Hiring: The 0.04% Acceptance Rate

Bending Spoons received approximately **600,000 applications in 2025** and hired around 50–100 new employees, yielding an acceptance rate of approximately **0.04%**. For context, Harvard's acceptance rate is around 3% — making Bending Spoons roughly 75x more selective.

The hiring process is described as **"4x to 10x the time investment"** of most tech companies. Ferrari's philosophy is that the quality of people is the single most important factor in the company's success: the better the people, the fewer you need, and the more each person can accomplish. He favors hiring young, less experienced candidates with exceptional raw potential over seasoned professionals with polished resumes but average capabilities.

The organic Rule of 40 — approximately 34 (13% organic growth + 21% margin) — is still respectable but much less impressive, suggesting a mature, modestly-growing business rather than a high-flying growth company.

## Module 7: The Bending Spoons Culture

### "Meritocracy" as Operating System

Bending Spoons describes its culture as a **radical meritocracy**. This isn't the vague "we hire the best people" platitude common in tech — it's a concrete operating principle that drives hiring, performance management, compensation, and separation decisions. At Bending Spoons, meritocracy means that every decision about a person's role, compensation, and continued employment is based on a **rigorous, data-driven assessment of their contribution** relative to what the company believes it could get from the external market.

This leads to one of the most distinctive — and polarizing — aspects of Bending Spoons' culture: the **annual assessment**. Each year, every employee is evaluated not just against their own past performance, but against **the hypothetical performance of the best external candidate** who could be hired for their role. If the company believes it could find someone better externally, the current employee may be at risk — regardless of their absolute performance level.

> **Warning:** ⚠️ The Assessment Philosophy
>
> This approach is fundamentally different from most performance management systems, which evaluate employees against goals or peer benchmarks. Bending Spoons evaluates against the **entire global talent market**. This creates an environment of perpetual competition — you're not just competing with your colleagues, you're competing with every person on earth who might want your job.

### Performance-Driven, Up-or-Out

The culture is explicitly **performance-driven with an up-or-out dynamic**. Employees are expected to continuously improve, take on increasing responsibility, and deliver measurable results. Those who plateau or underperform relative to the external benchmark face separation. This creates several cultural dynamics:

-   **High intensity:** The pressure to perform is constant and significant. Multiple Glassdoor reviews describe the environment as intellectually stimulating but relentlessly demanding.
-   **High turnover:** The assessment system, combined with the demanding work environment, creates turnover rates higher than industry averages. Glassdoor reviewers explicitly cite "excessively high turnover rate creating instability" as a con.
-   **Self-selection:** The culture attracts people who thrive under pressure and competition, and quickly filters out those who don't. This creates a cohort effect — the remaining team tends to be highly motivated and capable, which reinforces the culture.
-   **Young workforce:** The preference for hiring young, high-potential candidates (50–100 new hires annually from 600,000 applicants) means the company skews young, which aligns with the up-or-out dynamic — there aren't many 20-year veterans.

### Remote-First, Global Hiring

Bending Spoons operates as a **remote-first company** with its operational hub in Milan. While the company has a strong Milan presence (and the shared services functions are centralized there), product team members can work from anywhere. This remote-first approach serves the operating model in several ways: it allows the company to hire from a global talent pool (contributing to the 0.04% acceptance rate), it reduces real estate costs, and it makes post-acquisition transitions smoother since acquired employees can continue working remotely during the transition period (however brief that period may be).

### Compensation Model

Bending Spoons compensates at the **top of the market**. The company's philosophy is that paying well above market rates is not a cost — it's an investment that enables the small-team operating model. If you're going to run Evernote with 15 engineers instead of 200, those 15 engineers need to be exceptional, and exceptional people command exceptional compensation.

The compensation model likely includes significant equity participation, which the IPO will make more liquid. For the ~800 core employees, the company's $18.4 billion IPO valuation means even modest equity stakes translate to meaningful wealth — further reinforcing the attraction and retention of top talent.

### The Cultural Paradox

Bending Spoons presents a cultural paradox that's worth sitting with. The **internal** culture — for the ~800 core employees who survive the hiring gauntlet — appears to be genuinely excellent: high compensation, talented colleagues, significant ownership, meritocratic advancement, transparent leadership. The 96% "would recommend" rating on Glassdoor is real.

But the **external** impact of that culture — on the thousands of employees who are laid off after acquisitions, on the users whose products get stripped down and repriced, on the communities that lose the teams who built products they love — is much more complicated. This tension between internal excellence and external disruption is the defining characteristic of Bending Spoons' cultural identity.

## Module 8: Comparison to Other Tech Acquirers

### The Acquirer Landscape

Bending Spoons isn't the first company to build a business model around acquiring and optimizing software companies. But it's doing it differently enough from the established players to warrant careful comparison. Understanding where Bending Spoons fits — and where it diverges — is essential for evaluating the sustainability of its model.

### vs. Constellation Software (Mark Leonard)

**Constellation Software**, led by the legendarily private CEO Mark Leonard, is often cited as the gold standard of software acquisition roll-ups. Founded in 1995, Constellation has made **hundreds of small acquisitions** of vertical market software companies — obscure products that dominate tiny niches like marina management, cemetery operations, or library systems.

💡 The Key Difference

Bending Spoons' approach is faster, more aggressive, and largely unproven at scale.

The most important structural difference is **target size**. Constellation can grow by doing hundreds of small deals each year — there's an essentially unlimited supply of small vertical-market software companies. Bending Spoons is buying large, well-known brands. The supply of "mature consumer SaaS products with loyal audiences available at distressed valuations" is finite. Critics argue Bending Spoons is **"trapped buying bigger and bigger targets just to keep the growth story alive"** — a dynamic that Constellation avoided by staying down-market.

**Vista Equity Partners**, founded by Robert F. Smith, is one of the most successful private equity firms focused on enterprise software. Vista's playbook involves buying enterprise SaaS companies, applying operational rigor (through their value creation team), improving margins, and ultimately selling or taking public.

The key distinction is that Vista applies **operational improvements** to already-functioning organizations — making good companies better. Bending Spoons applies **radical transformation** — fundamentally rebuilding the operating model. Vista keeps most employees and optimizes processes; Bending Spoons replaces most employees with its own platform and people.

### vs. Thoma Bravo

**Thoma Bravo** is another major PE firm focused on software, but with a heavier emphasis on **financial engineering**. Thoma Bravo's playbook includes consolidation (merging acquisitions together), pricing optimization, and operational improvements, but the primary lever is often financial structure — leverage, tax optimization, and capital allocation.

Bending Spoons shares Thoma Bravo's willingness to use leverage but differs in that the value creation is fundamentally **operational and technological** rather than financial. Bending Spoons argues that their proprietary technology platform creates value that no financial buyer can replicate — and the revenue-per-employee numbers support this claim.

### vs. IAC / Barry Diller

**IAC (InterActiveCorp)**, led by Barry Diller, also builds a portfolio of internet companies, but with a fundamentally different approach. IAC acquires or incubates internet companies, grows them, and then **spins them off** as independent public companies — Match Group, Angi, Vimeo (ironically, before Bending Spoons bought it), Dotdash Meredith. IAC's model is about nurturing growth and creating standalone value, not operational integration and cost cutting.

Bending Spoons' Distinctive Advantages

The Universal OS gives them a genuine technological moat that PE firms can't replicate. No other acquirer can replace 70–85% of headcount with a shared platform.

Their 80% automation claim (even if exaggerated) represents a capability that traditional PE operational playbooks don't include.

### The European Dimension: Why Milan Matters

Bending Spoons' location in Milan is more strategically significant than it might appear. Most serial software acquirers are based in North America — Constellation in Toronto, Vista in San Francisco, Thoma Bravo in Chicago. Operating from Europe gives Bending Spoons several structural advantages:

-   **Cost arbitrage:** Even at premium European compensation, the all-in cost of a Milan-based engineer is lower than a San Francisco equivalent, especially when factoring in office costs, healthcare, and equity expectations. This isn't about hiring cheap — it's about getting elite talent at 70-80% of Silicon Valley cost.
-   **Less competitive deal market:** The European software acquisition market is less picked-over than the US market. While every US-based SaaS company with declining growth gets bombarded by PE offers, European targets like Komoot, WeTransfer, and Tractive may have fewer suitors, enabling better deal terms.
-   **Regulatory familiarity:** Operating within the EU gives Bending Spoons deep familiarity with GDPR, the Digital Services Act, and European labor law — increasingly important for any software company serving European users.
-   **Cultural cohesion:** Centralizing the core team in one city creates cultural density that's harder to achieve in a fully distributed model. The Milan hub becomes the beating heart of the company's operating culture.

The IPO listing on Nasdaq (rather than a European exchange) is strategic: it gives Bending Spoons access to the deepest capital markets in the world, the most sophisticated tech investors, and the highest comparable valuations. They get the best of both worlds — European operations with American capital market access.

10-30% headcount reduction. New KPIs and operational rigor. Some leadership changes. Most of the team survives if they adapt to the new metrics-driven culture.

##### Thoma Bravo

Variable — depends on the specific deal. Financial restructuring is primary focus. Team impact depends on the margin improvement plan. May be merged with other portfolio companies.

The risk, as noted by several analysts, is that Bending Spoons is like a **"Formula 1 car on a rain-slicked track"** — incredibly fast and technically impressive, but one slip could be catastrophic. The leverage, the integration complexity, the dependence on continued acquisition supply — any of these could create problems that the more conservative Constellation model would never face.

## Module 9: Lessons for Tech Leaders

### Lesson 1: Your Product Probably Has More Pricing Power Than You Think

The most universally applicable lesson from Bending Spoons is about **pricing courage**. Most software companies — and most VPs of Engineering and Product — systematically underestimate what their users are willing to pay. This happens for several reasons: founders are often builders who feel guilty charging for their creation, product teams optimize for growth metrics (users, MAU) rather than revenue metrics, and most organizations never run the aggressive pricing experiments that would reveal the true ceiling.

Bending Spoons' Evernote experience — raising prices 257% while retaining enough subscribers to grow revenue — challenges the fundamental assumption that drives most SaaS pricing strategies. Now, Bending Spoons has an advantage here: they bought a product with deeply embedded user habits and high switching costs. A note-taking app that holds years of your notes is extremely sticky. But the principle applies more broadly than most leaders realize.

💡 Action Item for VPs of Engineering

Ask your product team: When was the last time we ran a meaningful pricing experiment? Not a 10% increase — a 50% or 100% increase for a segment of users. If the answer is "never" or "years ago," you may be leaving significant revenue on the table. The engineering cost of running pricing experiments is low; the revenue impact can be transformative.

### Lesson 2: Most Software Teams Are Significantly Overstaffed

This is the hardest lesson for engineering leaders to hear, but Bending Spoons' track record makes it impossible to ignore. When a company takes a 300-person engineering team down to 30 people and the product **keeps working** — and in some cases improves — it forces a reckoning with a uncomfortable question: **What were those other 270 people doing?**

The honest answer, in many cases, is: maintaining complexity. As organizations grow, they create work to justify their size. Features nobody uses need to be maintained. Internal tools need to be built for processes that shouldn't exist. Code reviews create bottlenecks that require more people to resolve. Meetings proliferate to coordinate the growing number of people. Each additional engineer adds both capacity and coordination cost, and eventually the coordination cost exceeds the capacity gain.

Bending Spoons' model suggests that the optimal engineering team size for a mature product is much smaller than most organizations assume — perhaps **10–20% of what most companies employ**. This doesn't mean you should fire 80% of your team. But it does mean you should critically examine whether your team's headcount is driven by genuine product needs or by organizational entropy.

#### The "What Would Bending Spoons Do?" Exercise

Try this thought exercise with your leadership team: **If Bending Spoons acquired your product tomorrow, what would they cut?** Which features would they kill? Which teams would they eliminate? Which processes would they automate? This exercise is uncomfortable, but it reveals the gap between your current operating model and what's theoretically possible. You don't have to act on everything the exercise reveals — some of what Bending Spoons would cut has genuine value that they choose to sacrifice. But the exercise clarifies where you're carrying dead weight.

### Lesson 3: AI Automation Is an Engineering Strategy, Not Just a Feature

Most engineering organizations think about AI as a product feature — something you add to the product for users. Bending Spoons thinks about AI as an **operational strategy** — something that replaces the need for employees. This framing shift is critical.

When Bending Spoons says 80% of routine tasks are automated by AI, they're talking about internal operations: bug detection, customer support, content moderation, code generation, infrastructure management. Each AI capability they build reduces the number of humans needed to operate the portfolio. This is fundamentally different from adding AI features to the product (though they do that too, notably with Remini).

AI Automation Maturity Model for Engineering Orgs

##### Level 1: AI as Feature

AI features in the product (chatbots, recommendations). Most companies are here. Doesn't change headcount.

##### Level 2: AI-Assisted Dev

Engineers use Copilot/ChatGPT for coding. 10-20% productivity gain. Many companies are here.

##### Level 3: AI-Automated Ops

AI handles support, monitoring, bug triage, testing, deployments. Reduces ops headcount 30-50%. Few companies are here.

##### Level 4: AI-Native Org (Bending Spoons)

AI replaces 80% of routine tasks across the entire org. 5-10x efficiency. Almost no one is here yet.

### Lesson 4: Shared Platforms Create Compounding Advantages

Bending Spoons' Universal OS is the key to their operating model. The same infrastructure, the same billing system, the same analytics pipeline, the same support tools serve every product in the portfolio. Each new product added to the platform has a near-zero marginal operating cost because the shared infrastructure is already built and running.

For engineering leaders, this suggests a strategic investment thesis: **build internal platforms that reduce the marginal cost of operating each additional product or service**. If your company operates multiple products (or could in the future), the most valuable engineering work may not be product features — it may be the shared infrastructure that makes each product cheaper to run.

### Lesson 5: Revenue Per Engineer Is the Metric That Matters

Most engineering organizations track metrics like velocity (story points per sprint), cycle time, deployment frequency, or lines of code. These metrics measure **engineering activity**. Bending Spoons focuses on **engineering impact**, as measured by revenue per engineer.

With ~$2M in revenue per core employee, Bending Spoons is in the same efficiency tier as Apple and Google — companies with fundamentally more valuable product categories. This metric forces a different kind of prioritization. Every engineering decision is evaluated through the lens of: does this increase revenue relative to engineering cost?

💡 Benchmark Your Team

Calculate your team's revenue per engineer. Take your product's or business unit's annual revenue and divide by the number of engineers (including QA, DevOps, and engineering management). If the number is below $500K, you're in the bottom quartile of SaaS efficiency. Between $500K–$1M is good. Above $1M is excellent. Above $2M is Bending Spoons territory. This doesn't mean you should target $2M — the number depends on your product category and growth stage. But knowing where you stand is the first step.

### Lesson 6: When to Apply and When NOT to Apply This Model

Not every lesson from Bending Spoons translates to every context. The model works best when:

When the Bending Spoons Model Applies

##### ✔ Good Fit

Mature products with established user bases. Products with high switching costs. Consumer/prosumer subscription products. Products with untested pricing power. Organizations with high headcount relative to revenue.

##### ✘ Poor Fit

Early-stage products still finding PMF. Products competing on innovation speed. Enterprise products requiring deep customer relationships. Products where community and developer ecosystem are the moat. Companies where engineering talent IS the product (consulting, agencies).

### Lesson 7: Could Your Product Be "Bending Spoons'd"?

As a VP of Engineering, it's worth asking: **is your product a potential Bending Spoons target?** If any of the following are true, you might be vulnerable:

-   Your company is publicly traded at a low revenue multiple (1–3x revenue)
-   Your engineering team has grown faster than your revenue
-   Your product has a loyal, habitual user base that hasn't been tested with price increases
-   Your product could be maintained with significantly fewer engineers if the codebase were modernized
-   Your company has never seriously invested in AI automation of internal operations
-   Your product is a "good but broken" category leader — great brand, weak financials
-   Your founder/CEO is tired and looking for an exit

If three or more of these apply, you should be thinking about proactive efficiency improvements before someone else does it for you — with much less concern for the human impact.

### Lesson 8: The "Do More With Less" Philosophy in Practice

The past several years have seen "efficiency" become a mantra across tech, from Zuckerberg's "year of efficiency" to broad industry layoffs. Bending Spoons represents the **extreme logical conclusion** of this philosophy. They prove it's possible to run massive products with tiny teams. But they also demonstrate the tradeoffs:

-   **Speed of feature development slows.** A 15-person team simply can't ship as many features as a 200-person team, regardless of how talented they are.
-   **Innovation becomes incremental.** The focus shifts from "build the future" to "optimize what exists."
-   **Institutional knowledge is fragile.** With so few people, losing even one key engineer can be critical.
-   **Technical debt accumulates differently.** Instead of legacy code debt, you accumulate "breadth debt" — too many products for too few people to deeply understand.

The lesson isn't that you should become Bending Spoons. It's that the **spectrum between "Silicon Valley bloat" and "Bending Spoons austerity" is wider than most leaders realize**, and most companies are positioned far closer to the bloated end than the efficient end. Moving meaningfully toward efficiency — without going to Bending Spoons extremes — could transform your economics while preserving the cultural values and product quality that matter to you.

### Lesson 9: Building an AI-First Engineering Organization

Bending Spoons provides a roadmap for what an **AI-first engineering organization** actually looks like in practice — not as a marketing buzzword, but as an operational reality. For VPs of Engineering looking to move their teams toward AI-native operations, here's a practical framework inspired by Bending Spoons' approach:

#### Start with Support and Operations

The lowest-risk, highest-ROI place to deploy AI automation is customer support and operational tasks. Bending Spoons handles ~80% of support inquiries with AI across 50+ products. Most engineering organizations can achieve significant savings by deploying AI for: tier-1 support ticket routing and resolution, on-call alert triage (separating signal from noise), routine bug classification and prioritization, documentation generation and maintenance, and incident post-mortem summaries. None of these applications risk product quality, and all of them directly reduce headcount needs in support and operations teams.

#### Move to Code-Level Automation

The next frontier is automating aspects of the engineering workflow itself. This goes beyond individual developers using Copilot — it means organizational-level automation: automated code review for common patterns and anti-patterns, automated test generation for new code, automated dependency updates with regression testing, automated performance monitoring and optimization suggestions, and automated security scanning with remediation proposals. Bending Spoons' emphasis on modern codebases makes this possible — legacy code is harder for AI to work with. If your codebase is old, the highest-leverage investment may be modernization, specifically because it unlocks AI-powered automation.

#### Build the Shared Platform

Finally, invest in a shared platform that reduces the per-product operational burden. This is the Universal OS concept applied to your organization. Common services that every product needs — authentication, billing, analytics, experimentation, deployment — should be built once and shared. Each service added to the platform reduces the marginal cost of operating each product and creates a foundation for AI automation.

### Lesson 10: The CEO-CTO Alignment Imperative

One underappreciated aspect of Bending Spoons' success is the **deep alignment between business strategy and technology execution**. Ferrari isn't a non-technical CEO delegating to a CTO — he's an engineer (with degrees in information engineering and electronic engineering) who understands technology deeply enough to make integrated business-technology decisions. This alignment enables the aggressive moves that define Bending Spoons: the decision to rewrite codebases (a technology call with enormous business implications), the decision to replace teams with AI (a business call that requires deep technical understanding), the decision to build a shared platform (a multi-year technology investment that only pays off if the acquisition strategy succeeds).

For VPs of Engineering, the lesson is about **closing the gap between technical execution and business strategy**. The most effective engineering leaders don't just build what they're told to build — they shape the business strategy by bringing technical possibilities to the strategic conversation. Could you run your product with half the team if you invested in AI automation? Could you increase margins by modernizing the codebase? Could you create a platform that enables new product lines? These are engineering-led strategic insights that most VPs don't bring to the executive table because they're not thinking about engineering as a business lever.

## Module 10: Critical Analysis: Bull Case vs. Bear Case

### Is This Model Sustainable?

The core question for anyone evaluating Bending Spoons — as a potential investor, competitor, acquisition target, or case study — is whether this operating model is **sustainable at scale over the long term**. The honest answer is: we don't know yet. The company has been doing major acquisitions for only about four years, and the IPO is happening right now (July 2026). The model has generated impressive short-term results, but several fundamental questions remain unanswered.

### The Bull Case

#### 1\. The Math Works

Revenue has grown from $387M to a $2.4B run-rate in three years. Operating margins are healthy at ~20%. Revenue per employee is world-class at ~$2M. The Universal OS creates genuine operational leverage — each new product added to the platform has low marginal cost. These aren't vanity metrics; they represent real economic value creation.

#### 2\. The Acquisition Supply Is Deep

There are thousands of SaaS and digital products globally that fit Bending Spoons' target profile: established brands, loyal users, bloated operations, tired founders, depressed valuations. The addressable market for "good but broken" software companies is much larger than what Bending Spoons has tapped so far. The recent acquisitions of AOL ($1.5B) and Tractive ($900M) show they can move into new categories (legacy media, IoT hardware-adjacent) beyond pure SaaS.

#### 3\. AI Makes the Model Better Over Time

As AI capabilities improve, Bending Spoons' ability to automate operations improves too. The 80% automation they claim today could be 90% or 95% within a few years. This means the same 800-person core team could operate an even larger portfolio. The AI investment compounds — every automation built for one product can be applied to all products in the portfolio.

#### 4\. Subscription Revenue Is Resilient

Bending Spoons' revenue is 84–95% subscription-based. Subscription revenue is among the most predictable and resilient revenue types, providing a stable base for servicing the $4.4B debt load. The 94% net revenue retention, while below enterprise SaaS benchmarks, shows that subscribers are sticky despite price increases.

#### 5\. The European Advantage

Operating from Milan with a European workforce gives Bending Spoons a cost advantage over Silicon Valley-based acquirers, even at premium European compensation. This structural advantage enables the high revenue-per-employee metrics and supports the aggressive acquisition math.

### The Bear Case

#### 1\. The Debt Is Dangerous

$4.4 billion in debt is an enormous burden. Interest expense of $143M in 2025 grew at 337% year-over-year — more than three times faster than revenue. By Q1 2026, interest was consuming **78% of operating income**. In a rising rate environment, or if acquisition integration stumbles and revenue growth slows, the debt could become unserviceable. The S&P B+ credit rating — deep in junk territory — reflects this risk.

> **Warning:** ⚠️ The Leverage Trap
>
> The math is simple but scary: if interest expense continues growing faster than operating income, the company eventually faces a debt spiral. The IPO proceeds ($1.68B) are earmarked for acquisitions, not deleveraging. If the next several acquisitions don't generate the expected cash flows quickly enough, the balance sheet could become a crisis.

#### 2\. Organic Growth Is Weak

Only ~13 percentage points of 2025's 95% growth was organic. This means the existing portfolio of products is growing at a modest rate — roughly in line with GDP growth. Without continuous acquisitions, Bending Spoons would be a low-growth, heavily-indebted software portfolio. The "growth company" narrative depends entirely on the M&A pipeline, and any disruption to that pipeline (competition for deals, valuation inflation, regulatory scrutiny) could collapse the growth story.

#### 3\. The Acquisition Treadmill

To maintain the growth rates that justify its $18.4B valuation, Bending Spoons needs to **continuously acquire larger and larger targets**. In 2022, they were buying Evernote for an undisclosed amount (likely $100–200M). By 2025, they're buying AOL for $1.5B and Vimeo for $1.38B. The deals are getting bigger because the revenue base is bigger and larger acquisitions are needed to move the needle. This is the same treadmill that eventually trips up most serial acquirers: at some point, the targets large enough to matter become too expensive or too scarce.

#### 4\. Product Quality Degradation

There's growing evidence that the Bending Spoons playbook degrades product quality over time. Evernote user forums are filled with complaints about reduced functionality and support. Komoot's community is furious about the elimination of free features. Vimeo's entire video engineering team was eliminated. When you run a video platform without video engineers, or a note-taking app without a full product team, the product may continue to function in the short term but is unlikely to innovate or even maintain quality standards over multiple years.

The risk is a **slow erosion of the brand value** that made these products worth acquiring in the first place. If Evernote becomes synonymous with "overpriced, under-supported note app," the brand premium that supports the high pricing eventually evaporates.

#### 5\. Regulatory and Reputational Risk

As Bending Spoons grows and its pattern of post-acquisition layoffs becomes more widely known, it faces increasing **regulatory and reputational risk**. European labor laws are stricter than U.S. laws, and some of the acquired companies (Komoot in Germany, WeTransfer in the Netherlands) operate in jurisdictions with strong worker protections. The Follow the Money investigation and increasing media scrutiny could lead to regulatory attention, particularly in the EU.

There's also a growing risk that potential acquisition targets will **refuse to sell to Bending Spoons** because of its reputation. Founders who care about their employees and their product's legacy may prefer lower offers from acquirers who won't immediately gut the team. This could reduce the deal flow that the model depends on.

#### 6\. Key Person Risk

The entire model is deeply tied to **Luca Ferrari** and the founding team. The dual-class share structure gives them permanent control. The culture is shaped by their philosophy. The acquisition strategy reflects their judgment. If Ferrari were to depart or become incapacitated, it's unclear whether the model could continue — the bench depth behind the founders is unknown to outside observers.

#### 7\. The "When the Music Stops" Scenario

What happens when there are no more mature SaaS products to acquire at reasonable valuations? This could happen because: (a) Bending Spoons has already acquired the best targets; (b) competing acquirers bid up prices; (c) founders refuse to sell; or (d) the supply of "good but broken" companies naturally diminishes. If the M&A pipeline dries up and the existing portfolio is growing at only ~13% organically with $4.4B in debt and ~78% of operating income going to interest, the financial model becomes extremely precarious.

### The Comparison: What History Teaches Us About Roll-Up Models

Software roll-ups are not new. History provides several instructive parallels for evaluating Bending Spoons' trajectory:

#### Valeant Pharmaceuticals (now Bausch Health)

The most cautionary parallel. Valeant pursued an aggressive acquisition-and-cost-cutting strategy in pharmaceuticals: buy established drug brands, gut R&D, raise prices aggressively, use heavy leverage. The strategy generated spectacular growth until it didn't. When growth slowed and the debt burden became unmanageable, Valeant's stock dropped from $262 to $8. The parallels to Bending Spoons are uncomfortable: aggressive leverage, heavy cost-cutting, pricing power as the primary growth lever, and reliance on continuous acquisitions to maintain the growth narrative. The key question is whether Bending Spoons' technology platform creates genuine operational value that Valeant never had, making the comparison superficial rather than predictive.

#### 3G Capital / Kraft Heinz

3G Capital's approach to consumer goods companies — acquire, apply "zero-based budgeting," cut costs to the bone — generated impressive short-term results but eventually hit a wall. The cost-cutting damaged brand investment, product innovation, and employee morale to the point where organic growth collapsed. Kraft Heinz took a $15.4 billion write-down in 2019. The lesson: cost-cutting has a half-life. You can cut your way to profitability, but you can't cut your way to sustainable growth. Whether Bending Spoons' technology platform (AI, Universal OS, shared services) breaks this pattern — by replacing headcount with automation rather than simply eliminating functions — is the central question of the bull case.

#### Constellation Software: The Positive Model

On the optimistic side, Constellation Software has proven that a disciplined serial acquirer can compound value for decades. Constellation has returned over 15,000% since its 2006 IPO. The key differences that make Constellation more sustainable: lower leverage, smaller deal sizes (reducing integration risk), decentralized operations (keeping acquired teams intact), and a focus on vertical market software with natural monopoly characteristics. If Bending Spoons can execute with Constellation's discipline while maintaining the efficiency advantages of its technology platform, the upside is enormous. But the leverage and integration intensity make that a big "if."

### What the Market Is Pricing In

At an $18.4 billion IPO valuation on trailing twelve-month revenue of $1.6 billion, Bending Spoons is trading at approximately **~8x EV/revenue after adjusting for debt**. For context:

| Company | EV/Revenue | Growth Rate | Profile |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Bending Spoons | ~8x | 95% (mostly inorganic) | Serial acquirer, 21% op margin |
| Constellation Software | ~4.5x | ~20–25% | Proven 30-year track record |

| Typical SaaS (20% growth) | ~5–7x | ~20% | Organic growth |

The market is pricing Bending Spoons at a premium to Constellation despite a much shorter track record and higher leverage. This premium implies the market believes either: (a) the growth rate will sustain at high levels for several more years; (b) the technology platform creates margin expansion potential beyond what's currently visible; or (c) the IPO benefited from narrative momentum and investor enthusiasm that may not persist. Time will tell which interpretation is correct.

### Scenario Analysis: Three Paths Forward

Three Scenarios for Bending Spoons (2026–2031)

##### Bull Case (30% probability)

Acquisitions continue at pace. Universal OS creates genuine margin expansion. AI automation enables the same 800 people to run 100+ products. Revenue exceeds $5B by 2030. Leverage decreases as cash flow scales. Stock doubles from IPO. The "Constellation of consumer SaaS" narrative proves correct.

##### Base Case (45% probability)

Growth slows as large targets become scarce. Organic growth remains modest (~10-15%). Some acquired products degrade in quality, creating brand risk. Leverage gradually decreases but remains elevated. Revenue reaches $3-4B by 2030. Stock trades roughly flat from IPO. A solid but not exceptional business.

##### Bear Case (25% probability)

Integration stumble on a major acquisition. Rising interest rates squeeze margins. Product quality degradation leads to accelerating churn. Acquisition pipeline dries up. Organic growth stalls. Debt becomes a crisis. Stock falls 50%+. The Valeant analogy proves prescient.

### The Verdict

Bending Spoons is one of the most fascinating companies in tech — a genuine original that has created an operating model unlike anything else in the industry. The financial results are impressive. The efficiency is real. The technology platform is a genuine competitive advantage. And Luca Ferrari's strategic thinking is clearly several steps ahead of most CEOs.

But the model carries **significant risks** that increase, not decrease, with scale. The leverage is aggressive. The organic growth is modest. The product quality trajectory is concerning. The acquisition treadmill requires ever-larger deals. And the human cost — thousands of jobs eliminated, communities disrupted, products stripped down — is real and growing.

For tech leaders, the right response isn't to copy Bending Spoons or dismiss them. It's to **learn from the specific mechanisms that drive their efficiency** — shared platforms, AI automation, pricing courage, small teams — while being thoughtful about which tradeoffs you're willing to make. Not every team needs to be 15 people. Not every product needs to be repriced at 3x. Not every function needs to be automated. But the gap between where most organizations are and where they could be is larger than most leaders want to admit.

The Bending Spoons story is still being written. The IPO is happening today. In five years, we'll know whether this was the birth of a generational company or the peak of an unsustainable model. Either way, the lessons it offers are already worth studying.

💬 Final Thought

"Calculate boldly, but act rationally." — Luca Ferrari's company mantra, which captures both the ambition and the discipline that define Bending Spoons.

### The Edtech Angle: What This Means for Coursera, Canvas, and EdTech

For leaders at companies like Coursera, the Bending Spoons model raises an important strategic question: **could your company be a Bending Spoons target?** EdTech companies share several characteristics with typical Bending Spoons targets: strong brands with loyal user bases, subscription revenue models, often-bloated cost structures relative to revenue, and products where the core value proposition (content, credentials, learning outcomes) doesn't require massive engineering teams to maintain.

The defensive playbook for EdTech leaders watching Bending Spoons is clear: proactively apply the efficiency lessons before someone else forces them. Invest in AI-driven student support to reduce the support cost per learner. Build shared platform infrastructure that reduces the per-course operational cost. Test pricing elasticity — many EdTech products are underpriced relative to their career impact value. And most importantly, ensure your growth rate and margin profile don't make you look like a "good but broken" target. Companies that are growing quickly with expanding margins don't get acquired by Bending Spoons — companies that are stagnant with bloated costs do.

The silver lining is that EdTech has characteristics that make the pure Bending Spoons playbook harder to apply. Education has stronger regulatory requirements, accreditation dependencies, and content licensing relationships that can't be eliminated the way a customer support team can. The relationship between instructors, content, and learners creates a three-sided marketplace dynamic that requires more ongoing investment than a single-product subscription tool. And the social mission of education creates reputational risks for aggressive cost-cutting that don't exist when optimizing a file-transfer service or a photo editing app.
